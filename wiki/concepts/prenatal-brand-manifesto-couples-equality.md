# The Edit Brand Manifesto — TTC Is a Team Job

**Locked 2026-04-14.** The soul of [[prenatal-brand-name-the-edit|The Edit]] is a values position about reproductive equality.

## The manifesto

> "TTC is a team job. Men need to be equally invested."

In India, when couples decide to have a baby, only one person is asked to prepare. She visits doctors, takes vitamins, changes her diet, monitors her cycle. He continues life unchanged. But conception is a team sport — sperm quality matters as much as egg quality. Stress affects both. Lifestyle affects both. The Edit is built for couples who believe making a baby is a shared project from Day 1.

## Why this works as a brand position

1. **Category-creating in India.** No Indian supplement brand says this. [[trimacare]] positions to women only. Ayurvedic brands are doctor-led. The Edit becomes the first to make couples reproductive equality the brand's reason to exist.
2. **Real cultural intervention.** Indian reproductive labor is brutally lopsided. A brand that says "you both need to show up" is doing something culturally meaningful, not just selling vitamins.
3. **Defensible over time.** Trimacare can copy a formula. They cannot credibly copy the equality positioning — it's against their entire DNA (doctor-prescribed, mother-as-patient model).
4. **Content marketing moat.** Every piece of content can be about reproductive partnership. Male fertility (massively underserved in India) becomes The Edit's content territory that competitors cannot enter without contradicting their existing positioning.

## What this commits the brand to

- **Couples bundle (Him + Her) at Day 1 launch — non-negotiable.** Delaying Him by 6 months would contradict the manifesto.
- **Marketing always shows partnership** — never woman-as-solo-patient
- **Content addresses male fertility** — sperm quality, antioxidants, lifestyle
- **Packaging is gender-neutral or matched-pair** — never female-coded only
- **Couples bundle is the default purchase**, individual products are an alternative
- **Subscription is dual-shipment** — both bottles arrive together

## What this rules out

- Launching Her-only at Day 1 (rejected version of [[prenatal-revised-launch-sequence|Scenario F]])
- Pure prenatal positioning (the brand is bigger than pregnancy)
- Doctor-prescribed pharma model (we go direct to couples, not via OB-GYN to woman)
- Female-only marketing imagery
- Pink-pastel "moms-only" aesthetic

## Customer realities to plan for

1. **Indian male behavior change is the hardest part.** Men don't typically buy supplements proactively. The real customer is the woman who buys the couples bundle and hands him his bottle. Packaging, copy, and product design must make HIS adoption easy: simple, no-nonsense, daily ritual.

2. **Cultural sensitivity is real.** Some segments will love the feminist framing. Others (more traditional families) may bristle. Target: urban metro educated 28-38 demographic. Don't try to appeal to everyone.

3. **PR and founder story matter.** This is now a values-driven brand. [[aishwarya-chandrasekaran|Aishu]] + the gynecologist co-founder become the faces. Why they started this matters for credibility.
